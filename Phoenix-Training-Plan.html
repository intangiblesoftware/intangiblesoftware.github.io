Phoenix TMB Training Plan

16-Week Progressive Hiking Program — Tour du Mont Blanc Preparation

How This Plan Works

The TMB averages ~3,000 ft of elevation gain per day for 11 consecutive days. Phoenix can replicate the gradient but not the altitude or duration. This plan builds toward that sustained effort by increasing weekly gain across four phases, introducing back-to-back hiking days, and progressively adding pack weight.

A typical training week includes two weekday hikes (Tuesday/Thursday suggested) and one longer weekend hike (Saturday), with an optional Sunday recovery hike that increases in importance as the plan progresses. Rest is as important as training — two full rest days per week minimum in Phases 1 and 2.

If this plan feels too easy in Phase 1, you can compress Phases 1–2 by skipping ahead or adding laps. If it feels too hard, repeat any week before moving forward.

Why Back-to-Back Days Matter

The TMB's hardest challenge for most hikers isn't any single day — it's hiking on legs that are already tired from the previous day. Starting in Phase 2, this plan introduces Saturday/Sunday paired hikes. Sunday hikes are deliberately scheduled after a hard Saturday to teach your body to move on tired legs. By Phase 3 you should feel the difference in how quickly you recover.

Gear Break-In Timeline

What to Have Ready and When
Week 1 Hiking boots on every hike — new or recently purchased. No blister surprises on the TMB. Resolve hot spots now.
Week 2–3 Try hiking poles. Decide whether you'll use them on the TMB. The descents on Stages 6, 9, 11 are where poles earn their weight.
Week 4–5 Finalize your hiking socks and insoles. If you're going to change them, change now — not the week before the TMB.
Week 6 Add 10 lb pack weight — use your actual TMB daypack loaded with the gear you'll carry each day (water, layers, snacks, first aid).
Week 9–10 Increase to 15–20 lb — your realistic TMB carry weight including layers for alpine weather, full water, and lunch.
Week 11–12 All gear should be dialed in. No experimental items after this point. Test your rain gear and gaiters on a wet or early-morning hike.
Week 13 Full gear audit. List anything that needs replacing. Last chance to order replacements with time to break them in.
Week 16 Pack your TMB bag and walk around the neighborhood with it for 30 minutes. Confirm weight and fit feel right before you board the plane.

Extending to 20 or 24 Weeks

This plan is built for a moderately active person with some hiking experience. If you want more time — either because you're building from a less active base, or because your TMB date is further out — add weeks to Phase 1 and Phase 2 before the intensity ramps up:

  • 20-week plan (add 4 weeks): Repeat Weeks 2 and 3, then repeat Weeks 6 and 7. Everything else stays the same. The extra weeks solidify your aerobic base before you start pushing volume.
  • 24-week plan (add 8 weeks): Add two weeks each to Phases 1, 2, and 3. Phase 1 weeks can be very easy — the goal is building the trail habit and boot break-in, not fitness. Phase 2 extra weeks can be used to add a fifth trail to your rotation. Phase 3 extra weeks can include a multi-day camping trip or a drive to higher elevation (Flagstaff, ~7,000 ft) for altitude exposure.
  • Altitude prep (bonus): If your TMB is more than 6 weeks away after Week 14, consider a weekend trip to Flagstaff (Humphreys Peak trail, ~10,000 ft) for a taste of real altitude. This won't fully acclimatize you but it's informative — you'll know how your body responds to thin air.

Phoenix Heat — Seasonal Training Adjustments

October–April: Ideal training conditions, flexible start times.
May–June: Start Camelback and Piestewa before 7:00 am. Siphon Draw before 6:00 am. Carry 1 liter per hour minimum.
July–September: Start Camelback/Piestewa by 5:30–6:00 am. Monsoon season begins mid-July — watch for afternoon lightning at elevation and flash floods in canyons. Siphon Draw can be hiked slightly later due to higher elevation (~5,000 ft) but still demands very early starts.
Year-round: Electrolytes matter more in the desert than in the Alps. Train with the same hydration products you'll use on the TMB.
